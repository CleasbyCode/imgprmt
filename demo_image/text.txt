Feeding friendly neighborhood cats can absolutely confuse them about their territory, often leading them to believe your property is their primary home.By providing regular meals, you are establishing a strong food resource, which is the primary driver for cats to expand or shift their home range. While they may still visit their original owners or roam their broader territory, they will quickly adjust their schedule to your feeding times and view your porch or yard as a core part of their living space.Key behavioral shifts include:Relocation: The cat may spend significantly less time at their actual home and more time waiting around your doors.Territorial conflicts: Attracting cats to a specific feeding spot can cause friction with other neighborhood or feral cats competing for the food.Overcrowding: If the cat belongs to a neighbor, your supplemental feeding can lead to overfeeding, which poses health risks like obesity.To ensure the cat's health and happiness without causing confusion, it is best to coordinate with neighbors before taking on the role of a secondary feeder. You can check local animal welfare guidelines, such as those from the RSPCA, for best practices on managing community or outdoor cats.
